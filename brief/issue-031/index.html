Pittsburgh took Houston down 10-6 — and did it by playing the kind of baseball that makes regular season records look like a lie. The Pirates weren't flashier or deeper. They just executed when it mattered, and Houston's offense couldn't find a rhythm all night. This is the thing nobody wants to admit about June baseball: one team remembers how to win in tight spots, and the other one doesn't yet.

Cleveland demolished New York 9-4, which on the surface looks like the Guardians finally showed up. But the real story is the Yankees' offense collapsing when it counted most — four runs in a game where they needed at least seven. That's not a bad night. That's a team that hasn't figured out how to score in rhythm.

Milwaukee handled San Francisco 8-3, the kind of comfortable road win that builds quiet confidence. Giants got run early and never recovered.

Why it matters: Two days from now, the NBA Finals tips off in San Antonio — Knicks against the Spurs, a matchup that will define someone's entire season. Everything baseball does between now and Friday is just noise. But if you're watching baseball because you love the sport, these three games told you something clean: Pittsburgh's the team nobody's afraid of right now, Cleveland's got something clicking, and the Yankees have a reckoning coming.

What you're saying tonight: "Pittsburgh just proved they belong. Houston looked lost." Or, if you watched the Yankees: "Four runs. That's not a playoff team doing playoff things."

Next: Watch Cleveland's lineup in their next matchup — if they keep swinging for contact instead of pulling everything, they've genuinely changed. If they go back to trying to hit homers, that win was just one good night.

Markets

Nvidia and Microsoft both crater as rates climb

The 10-year yield jumped 8 basis points overnight—mortgage territory again—and tech got the memo. Nvidia, Microsoft, and Dell all dropped 3%+ because higher rates make their future earnings worth less today, a math problem that doesn't improve until the Fed signals it's done tightening.

SPY dipped 0.7% on the noise, but here's what matters: Thursday brings CPI data, and the market's already priced in "hotter than expected." If inflation prints cool, you get a relief rally and tech rebounds hard. If it stays elevated, the 10-year keeps climbing and you're looking at the summer grind nobody wants to talk about yet. Your 401(k) should care less about today's red and more about whether the Fed actually pauses next month.

The 10-year yield jumped 80 basis points this week — watch Thursday's jobs report to see if the Fed's really worried about inflation or if this is just a summer wobble.

  • Celebrity
    Russo Bros Just Bet the Farm on RDJ's Comeback
    Hollywood Reporter

    The Russo Brothers didn't just announce 'Avengers: Doomsday' — they declared Marvel's entire playbook broken and are torching it to start over. Bringing Robert Downey Jr. back as a villain after a decade-plus isn't nostalgia casting; it's an admission that the multiverse era exhausted audiences, and the only way back in is through a character people actually believed in. The real risk isn't whether RDJ can still carry weight (he can) — it's that Marvel's telling us the MCU's momentum since Endgame has been hollow enough to warrant a complete reset. If this works, every studio's going to start mining their old A-listers for redemption arcs instead of building new ones.

  • Music
    Black Crowes Remind Florida Why 'U.S.A.' Chants Hit Different
    Rolling Stone

    A crowd started chanting 'U.S.A.' during a Black Crowes show in Florida, and the band shut it down hard — told them they weren't being ignorant, but that a chant shouldn't be a rallying cry at a rock concert when the country's fractured the way it is. That kind of pushback from a stage is rarer now; most acts either let the crowd do its thing or ignore it entirely. The Crowes' read was smarter: they separated patriotism from tribalism without preaching, and made the audience sit with the difference for a second. You don't have to agree with their call to recognize that a band willing to read a room and challenge it beats polite entertainment every time.

The Sharp Take

New York just walked into San Antonio for Game 1 of the Finals, and the Knicks' whole season comes down to one thing: can they play the kind of defense that got them here, or will Wembanyama's length make them regret every three-pointer they jacked up in the regular season? The Spurs are younger, longer, and built specifically to punish jump-heavy teams. This isn't about pace or spacing—it's about whether New York's half-court discipline holds for 48 minutes.

Watch the first six minutes. If New York's getting beat off the dribble or giving up cuts in transition, you'll know they're thinking too much and not trusting their reads. If they're locked in on the wings and forcing San Antonio into long twos, they can steal Game 1 and turn this into a series. The Spurs have one of the best defenses in the league, but they're not experienced in elimination basketball—that's the one edge the Knicks actually have.
