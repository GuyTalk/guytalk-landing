Trump ties Saudi nuclear deal to normalization with Israel

What happened: President Trump stated that a new U.S.–Saudi Arabia civilian nuclear agreement requires Riyadh to normalize relations with Israel under the Abraham Accords.

Why it matters: It links a major foreign policy and energy deal to geopolitical alignment—raising the stakes in Middle East diplomacy and U.S. strategic positioning.

The GuyTalk Read: This isn't just another headline—it's Trump using energy policy as political leverage. A nuclear deal on one hand, a diplomatic trigger on the other. That’s the kind of deal that gets people talking after work.

  • Announcement made July 23, 2026 by President Trump via social media and AP coverage
  • Affirms the lack of uranium enrichment, per the statement
  • One of the few US-Saudi deals conditioning normalization with Israel

What to say: “Trump is basically saying: ‘You want nuclear help? You gotta make friends with Israel first.’ That’s next‑level diplomacy.”

What happened
Ben Kohles holds the lead at the 3M Open at 9-under, two shots clear of Emiliano Grillo and Jackson Koivun, who are tied for second at 7-under.
Why it matters
Kohles has built real separation heading into the weekend, which matters because golf tournaments compress fast when the field tightens. A two-shot lead with 36 holes left is solid but not commanding—one hot round from Grillo or Koivun and this gets interesting.
Big moments
  • Kohles shot 9-under to open a two-shot lead over the field

Kohles is playing the cleaner golf right now, but this isn't over. Grillo especially has the pedigree to make a run—he's a former PGA Tour winner with a short memory for bad shots. The question isn't whether Kohles can hold on; it's whether he can extend. A 36-hole sprint favors hot putters, and we won't know who that is until Saturday.

Big moments

  • Film
    Sia's concert film finds her voice again

    What happened: A new review of 'Sia: Nostalgic for the Present' dropped today, highlighting the concert performance film and the deliberate artistic mystique she's built by keeping her face off camera and her personal life out of the spotlight.

    Why it matters: Sia's refusal to play the traditional celebrity game has made her more interesting, not less — and this film is proof that restraint is still a radical move in an era of total personal exposure.

    The GuyTalk Read: Sia has spent two decades proving you don't need your face on every screen to own a room. The concert film works because it lets the music and the performance do the talking while she controls the frame entirely. In a culture obsessed with personal branding and constant visibility, she's built something rarer: real mystique. This is what happens when an artist actually has a point of view about what they're selling.

    • Sia has performed with her back to cameras or face obscured for years, making anonymity her brand
    • She has written and produced hits for Rihanna, Katy Perry, and The Weeknd while staying largely anonymous herself
    • 'Nostalgic for the Present' is a concert film — not a documentary — keeping the focus on music and visual spectacle rather than personal narrative

    What to say: Sia basically proved you can be bigger by being less visible — this film is the whole argument in one package.

  • Streaming
    Black Bag is the spy thriller worth your weekend

    What happened: Steven Soderbergh's spy thriller Black Bag features Michael Fassbender and Cate Blanchett as intelligence agents who are married to each other — until one of them is suspected of being a mole.

    Why it matters: Soderbergh keeps the pace tight and the stakes personal — two people who know each other's every move, suddenly unsure who's lying. It's smart espionage without the three-hour Cold War lecture.

    The GuyTalk Read: If you liked the tension of Tinker Tailor Soldier Spy but didn't want to spend three hours decoding Cold War politics, this is the version for you. The concept is clean: the most dangerous person in the room is the one who already knows all your tells. Soderbergh is one of the few directors who can make a thriller feel like a conversation.

    What to say: Soderbergh made an espionage thriller that's actually about marriage — worth the watch.
