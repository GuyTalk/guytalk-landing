Now San Antonio gets the Finals, and they're the only team anyone wants to see right now. *Conversation line: Duncan's 34 in Game 7 closes his case against anyone who thinks this era belongs to the young guys.*

Nvidia soars as AI trade shrugs off broader weakness

**Markets** Nvidia's 4.3% rip on Friday pulled the Magnificent Seven in different directions—MSFT and NVDA are still hunting new highs while AAPL and TSLA got hit hard enough to matter. The real story is that the market's finally discriminating between AI winners and pretenders instead of treating them as one monolithic bet. Earnings season kicks into high gear next week, and the Street's spooked about guidance. Every tech CFO will be grilled on whether AI capex actually moves the needle, and one weak outlook could snap this fragile rally. Watch for margin pressure in cloud computing—if AWS or Azure growth rates disappoint, QQQ gets ugly fast. Bitcoin's 3.4% drop matters because it signals risk-off positioning ahead of the Fed's next move. One month out from potential rate cuts, institutions are taking chips off the table rather than chasing.

Fed speakers hit the circuit this week—watch for any signals on rate cuts after hotter-than-expected inflation data keeps the door closed through summer.

The Sharp Take

SPURS END THUNDER'S SEASON IN GAME 7

Gregg Popovich just reminded everyone why he's still coaching. The Spurs beat Oklahoma City 111-103 in a Game 7 that never got pretty but never needed to. San Antonio's defense held Shai Gilgeous-Alexander to 28 points on 40 percent shooting, and that was enough. Victor Wembanyama finished with 19 and 13, proving again that having a seven-footer who can actually move changes everything.

This was thunder's window closing. They were the second seed with the best chance to challenge Denver, and they couldn't finish it when Pop's roster had zero business being here. Popovich sent them home.

Conversation line: Spurs' bench outscored OKC's bench 24-6. That's not lucky.
