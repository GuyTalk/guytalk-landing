Fit Fundamentals

How clothes should actually fit on your body. The rules that make everything work.

Fit is the foundation. You can have the most expensive shirt in the world, and if it doesn't fit, you look sloppy. You can have a $40 shirt, and if it fits right, you look sharp. This is non-negotiable. Learn the rules, apply them to everything, and half your style problems disappear.

Shoulders: The Most Important Measurement

The shoulder seam should sit exactly at your shoulder bone. Not an inch forward, not an inch back. This is the one measurement that can't be fixed by a tailor. If shoulders are off, the whole thing is off. Everything else can be adjusted, but shoulders define the silhouette.
When you're trying something on, let your arms hang naturally and look at where the seam sits. It should match your shoulder point exactly. This is how you find your actual size.

Sleeves: The Second Line of Defense

Shirt sleeves should end at your wrist bone when your arms hang at your sides. About half an inch of wrist should show. Too long and you look like you're wearing your dad's clothes. Too short and you look like you're waiting for a flood.
For jackets, sleeves should show about half an inch of shirt cuff. Same principle — not drowning, not too short. This applies to blazers, sports coats, dress jackets. Everything.

The Body: Should Skim, Not Cling or Drown

Your shirt shouldn't be so tight that it outlines every ab (or lack thereof). It also shouldn't be so loose that you look like you're wearing a tent. Aim for this: if you pinch the fabric on the side of your torso, you should be able to grab about an inch of fabric comfortably. No more, no less.
This applies to casual shirts, t-shirts, dress shirts, everything. Skim your body without hugging it. That's the sweet spot.

Pants: Length and Taper

Length. Your pant should break slightly at the top of your shoe — a small fold where the fabric meets the shoe. Not dragging on the ground, not sitting too high on your ankle showing too much sock. When you're standing naturally, about 80 percent of your shoe should be visible.
Taper. Pants shouldn't be so baggy that they hide your legs or so tight that they cling. The leg should taper slightly as it goes down, following the line of your leg. Wide-leg cuts exist, but they're a style choice, not the default. Most guys look better in a tapered fit.
Waist. Pants should sit at your natural waist — the smallest part around your middle. Not sagging below your hips, not pulled up to your ribs. Comfort matters here, but your natural waist is usually the best fit.

The Fit Test

Before you buy anything, stand in front of a mirror and move. Raise your arms. Bend over. Sit down. Does it feel restrictive? Does it fit your frame? Do the sleeves ride up when you move? Does the shirt pull at the buttons?
If something feels wrong standing still, it'll feel worse moving around. Trust your gut. If you have to convince yourself it fits, it probably doesn't.

The Accessory Test

Once you think something fits, add a layer. Put on a blazer over the shirt. Does it still look good? Wear the pants with your normal shoes and belt. Does everything work together? Fit isn't just about individual pieces — it's about how they work as a whole outfit.

Key Takeaways

  • Shoulders are non-negotiable; they can't be tailored
  • Sleeves should end at your wrist bone
  • Shirt body should skim without clinging
  • Pants should break slightly at the shoe and taper naturally
  • Test fit by moving, not just standing still
  • When in doubt, fit beats brand every time
