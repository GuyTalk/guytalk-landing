# GuyTalk Editorial Bible

> This file is the single source of truth for GuyTalk editorial standards.
> The Claude editor pass (`scripts/lib/editor.js`) loads this file at runtime and
> enforces every rule below on each generated brief before it can preview or publish.
> Edit this file to change the standard — no code change required.

## Core identity

GuyTalk is not a generic newsletter. It is a social utility product.

The goal is not just to report what happened. The goal is to help the reader quickly understand what matters, why it matters, and what they can say about it in real life.

Every issue should feel like:
“Here’s what happened today, why people care, and how to talk about it without sounding lost.”

## Main rule

No topic earns a spot unless it gives the reader conversational value.

Every included item must answer at least two of these:

* Why does this matter?
* Why would people be talking about this?
* What is the simple takeaway?
* What could I say in a group chat, at work, at a bar, or around friends?

## Lead with the biggest story (organic, every day)

GuyTalk must be organic — it leads with whatever is genuinely the biggest thing happening that day, across ALL domains (markets, business, tech, world, sports, culture), not a fixed template and not whatever sports game happened to be on.

The facts you are given include a section, **"TODAY'S BIGGEST STORIES" (web-researched, real & sourced)**, with the single biggest story of the day marked **★**. When that ★ story is bigger than the day's sports result (a record IPO, a major ruling, a huge product launch, a major economic print, a war/peace development), it MUST lead the issue:

* The **headline** leads with it.
* The **Top Hits** lead with it (the Markets/Business hit is the ★ when it's a markets/business story).
* It gets full depth in its section (usually Markets/Business).

Never bury the day's biggest story under a regular-season game. A guy should never read the brief and be the last to know about the single biggest thing that happened.

## Required issue structure

Every brief must follow this order:

1. Top Hits
2. Key Walkaway
3. Markets
4. Sports
5. Business / Money / Media
6. Culture
7. Style / Lifestyle / Fun
8. What to Bring Up

## Top Hits rules

The top of every issue must have 4–6 quick bullets showing the biggest topic hits.

Each bullet should be short, punchy, and specific.

Bad:

* F1 Monaco happened

Good:

* Monaco gave F1 fans drama, strategy, and a prestige win people will actually remember.

## Key Walkaway rules

Every issue needs one short key summary before the reader gets into sections.

It should be 2–4 sentences max.

It should tell the reader the “vibe of the day.”

## Market rules

GuyTalk must never give investment advice, stock recommendations, price targets, buy/sell/hold language, or portfolio suggestions.

Allowed:

* Observing what markets did
* Explaining why sectors moved
* Mentioning major earnings, economic data, or company news
* Saying what investors are watching

Not allowed:

* “This could be a buying opportunity”
* “Investors should…”
* “This stock looks attractive”
* “Consider buying/selling”
* “This is a good long-term play”

Market language must be observational only.

### Market-event depth checklist (for a major market/business story)

When the day has a major market or business event — a big IPO, a major ruling, a hot economic print, a large M&A or earnings shock — the Markets section must actually ANSWER the questions a reader would ask, not just name it. Cover, in plain English and still observational (never advice):

1. **What happened** — the specifics (numbers, names, dates).
2. **Why it matters** — the bigger read-through.
3. **Market impact** — how it moves the rest of the market, sectors, or major indices (e.g. whether the S&P 500 would add a newly public company — and that index inclusion isn't automatic).
4. **How a regular person can participate** — if relevant (e.g. which brokerages, how to access it), framed as information, never a recommendation.
5. **Historical comparison** — how similar events have actually played out (weeks/months/a year later), with real examples.

The web-researched "DEPTH" note in the facts is there for exactly this — use it. Answering these is what makes GuyTalk worth reading over a headline.

Use phrases like:

* “Markets moved…”
* “Investors watched…”
* “The main read-through was…”
* “The concern was…”
* “The reaction suggested…”

## Sports rules

Never list a game or event without at least one specific detail.

Every sports item needs:

1. Result or main event
2. Key player, play, moment, or storyline
3. Why it matters or why it is worth bringing up

Bad:

* Reds beat the Cubs.

Good:

* Reds beat the Cubs 5–3 behind a late rally, with Elly De La Cruz creating pressure on the bases again. The bigger point: Cincinnati is giving fans something to actually track beyond just the final score.

F1 rule:
If Monaco, Silverstone, Miami, Vegas, or any major race is mentioned, explain why that race matters culturally or competitively. Monaco should never be treated like a normal race.

Golf rule:
If a tournament is mentioned, include the winner, notable contender, course/storyline, and why casual fans might care.

## Culture rules

Culture cannot feel like random headlines.

Every culture item should include:

* What happened
* Why people care
* What to say about it

Avoid vague celebrity filler unless there is a real conversation angle.

Bad:

* New trailer dropped.

Good:

* A new trailer dropped and immediately took over social feeds because fans are debating whether the studio is reviving the franchise or just recycling nostalgia.

## Tone

GuyTalk should sound smart, casual, confident, and useful.

It should not sound:

* Corporate
* Robotic
* Overly formal
* Like a financial advisor
* Like ESPN copy-paste
* Like a generic newsletter

Preferred tone:

* “Here’s the actual angle.”
* “The bigger thing is…”
* “This is why people care.”
* “That gives you something easy to bring up.”

## Length rules

Each item should usually be 2–5 sentences.

No giant paragraphs.

No section should feel like a wall of text.

If a topic needs more than 5 sentences, it probably needs to be tightened.

## Formatting rules

Keep sections visually consistent.

Each story should follow this general format:

### Topic headline

**What happened:**
**Why it matters:**
**What to bring up:**

This can be lightly adapted, but the logic must stay consistent.

## Final quality check

Before publishing, review every item and ask:

1. Is this useful in conversation?
2. Did we explain why it matters?
3. Is there at least one specific detail?
4. Is the wording clean and casual?
5. Did we avoid investment advice?
6. Does this feel like GuyTalk, not a generic newsletter?

If any answer is no, rewrite or remove the item.
